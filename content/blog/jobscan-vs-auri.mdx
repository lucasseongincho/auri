If you've been job searching for more than a week, you've probably heard of Jobscan. 
It's been around since 2014 and it does one thing well: it compares your resume against 
a job description and gives you a match score.

A 67%? Tweak your keywords. An 82%? You're probably good to apply.

It sounds useful. For a lot of job seekers, it is — at first. But after you've chased

**If my score is high, why am I still not hearing back?**

This article breaks down what Jobscan actually does, where it falls short, and why

the job search as a system — not a scoring game.

---

## What Jobscan Does Well

Let's be fair. Jobscan solves a real problem.

Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) are used by over 98% of Fortune 500 companies to 
filter resumes before a human ever reads them. These systems scan for keywords from 
the job description. If your resume doesn't contain the right terms — even if you're 
qualified — you can get filtered out automatically.

Jobscan helps you close that gap. Paste in your resume and a job description, and it 
highlights missing keywords, flags formatting issues, and gives you a percentage score 
indicating how well your resume matches.

For someone who has never thought about ATS optimization, this is genuinely eye-opening.

**Where Jobscan earns its reputation:**
- Keyword matching against job descriptions
- Identifying hard and soft skills gaps
- Basic formatting checks for ATS compatibility
- Resume parsing feedback

If you're starting from zero, Jobscan gives you a useful baseline.

---

## Where Jobscan Falls Short

Here's the problem: **ATS optimization is only the first filter.**

Getting past the ATS means your resume lands in a recruiter's inbox. That's it. 
It doesn't mean they'll read it carefully. It doesn't mean they'll call you. 
It definitely doesn't mean you'll get the job.

A resume that's been keyword-stuffed to hit an 85% match score often reads like 
a robot wrote it — because, in a sense, it was written *for* a robot. Recruiters 
notice this immediately.

Beyond that, Jobscan has no answer for some of the most common job search problems:

**What do you say in your cover letter?** Jobscan doesn't write one for you, 
and it doesn't tailor it to the specific role and company.

**How do you present yourself on LinkedIn?** Your LinkedIn profile is often the 
first thing a recruiter looks at after your resume. Jobscan doesn't touch it.

**What questions will they ask in the interview?** Getting an interview is only 
valuable if you're prepared for it. Jobscan's scope ends at the application.

**What's your actual job search strategy?** Which roles should you apply to? 
How many applications is the right number? How do you prioritize? Jobscan 
doesn't address any of this.

At $49.95/month, you're paying for a single tool that solves a single problem 
at a single stage of your job search.

## The Score Trap

There's a psychological issue with score-based tools that doesn't get talked 
about enough: **they make optimization feel like progress.**

You spend an hour tweaking your resume to go from a 71% to an 86% match. 
You feel productive. You've done something. But you haven't applied to any jobs. 
You haven't written a cover letter. You haven't reached out to anyone.

The score becomes the goal instead of the interview.

This is what we call the score trap — and it's one of the main reasons smart, 
qualified job seekers spend weeks "optimizing" and still have nothing to show for it.
